What is Project

Definition of Project

A Project is a temporary, unique and progressive attempt or endeavor made to produce some kind of a tangible or intangible result (a unique product, service, benefit, competitive advantage).

It usually includes a series of interrelated tasks that are planned for execution over a fixed period of time and within certain requirements and limitations such as cost, quality and performance.

Top 5 characteristics of a project:

  1. Change

    2. Project is a way to introduce change.

  2. Temporary

    3. Project has a finite start and a finite end. The start is the time when the project is initiated and its concept is developed. The end is reached when all objectives of the project have been met.

  3. Cross-Functional

    4. Projects always cut across the regular organizational lines and structures within a firm. It engages people from different seniority and business departments that work together for the period of the project.

  4. Unique

    5. It requires the doing of something different, something that was not done previously. A project is a one-time, once-off activity, never to be repeated exactly the same way again.

  5. Uncertainty

    6. Unnecessary time spent on contract issues due to preplanning problems, discussions with clients due to and the historic dependency of the project are all sources of deviations causing uncertainty and ambiguity that must be resolved in any project.

Key features of project and program

Project Program
Scope Projects have a narrow scope with specific deliverables Programs have a wide scope that may have to change to meet the benefit expectations of the organization
Measure of Success Success is measured by delivery of project deliverables per their specifications within the project budget and on time. Success is measured in terms of on-going achievement of the specific strategy or objective.
Role of Change Management Project Managers focus on organizational change as it impacts the project's end customers and stakeholders. Program Managers have to expect and embrace change.
Leadership Style Leadership style focuses on task delivery and directive in order to meet the success criteria. Leadership style focuses on providing vision and leadership, managing relationships and conflict resolution.
Organizational Leadership's Role Experienced managers lead projects and need to understand the triple constraints of scope, budget, and schedule Executive involvement, support and leadership is key to the success of programs as they span many organizational groups and have sustained resource requirements.
Level of Planning Project managers conduct detailed planning to manage the delivery of the products of the project. Program managers create high-level plans providing guidance to projects where detailed plans are created
Management Project managers manage project staff including technicians, subject matter experts and analysts. Program Managers manage project managers, and those supporting the implementation of program standards and processes.

Basic phases of Project

4 basics phases of Project Life Cycle:

  • Project Initiation

  • Project Planning

  • Project Execution

  • Project Closure
  • Project Initiation

    • Project Initiation activities are:

    1. Develop a Business Case

      2. It includes a description of the business problem or opportunity, the costs and benefits of each alternative solution, and the recommended solution for approval.

    2. Undertake a Feasibility Study

      3. Project managers use feasibility studies to determine potential positive and negative outcomes of a project before investing a considerable amount of time and money into it.

    3. Establish the Project Charter

      4. It describes the project vision, objectives, scope and deliverables, as well as the Stakeholders, roles and responsibilities.

    4. Appoint the Project Team

    5. Set up the Project Office

      6. The project office contains the communications infrastructure and technologies required to support the project.

    6. Perform a Phase Review

      7. During this project review, a decision is made as to whether or not the team has met the objectives and is approved to proceed to the next project phase, being the "planning" phase.

  • Project Planning

    • It involves creating of a set of plans to help guide your team through the execution and closure phases of the project. The plans will help you to manage time, cost, quality, change, risk and issues. They will also help you manage staff and external suppliers, to ensure that you deliver the project on time and within budget.

    1. Create a Project Plan
      • Define the project scope & milestones
      • Identify the Work Breakdown Structure
      • Set and agree the target delivery dates
      • Monitor and control the allocation of resource
      • Report on the progress to the sponsor
    2. Create a Resource Plan

      3. It specify the exact quantities of labor, equipment and materials needed to complete your project.

    3. Create a Financial Plan

      4. It defines all of the various types of expenses that a project will incur during each stage of the project. The financial plan also summarizes the total expense which becomes the project budget.

    4. Create a Quality Plan

      5. It comprises two parts:

      Quality Assurance Plan lists the independent reviews needed.

      Quality Control Plan lists the internal reviews needed to meet your quality targets.

    5. Create a Risk Plan

      6. It lists all predicted risks, their ranking and priority, the preventative and contingent actions, along with a process for tracking them.

    6. Create an Acceptance Plan

      7. The Acceptance Plan includes a list of the deliverables, the acceptance test activities, the criteria and standards to be met, and the plan for their completion.

    7. Create a Communications Plan

      8. Whenever you have a variety of staff, external suppliers, customers and stakeholders to communicate with, then you should record your communications formally in a Communication Plan.

    8. Create a Procurement Plan

      9. Using the Procurement Plan template, you can define the procurement requirements, identify potential suppliers, contract those suppliers and manage them to ensure deliverys.

    9. Contract the Suppliers

      10. This steps include: tender process, statement of work, request for nformation anp proposal, supplier contract, tender register.

  • Project Execution

    • This phase involves implementing the plans created during the project planning phase. To ensure that the customer's requirements are met, the project manager monitors and controls the activities, resources and expenditure required to build each deliverable. Each project deliverable produced should be reviewed for quality and measured against the acceptance criteria.

  • Project Closure

    • The Project Closure phase involves releasing the final deliverables to the customer, handing over project documentation, terminating supplier contracts, releasing project resources and communicating project closure to all stakeholders. The last remaining step is to conduct lessons-learned studies to examine what went well and what didn’t, which can help future project teams.

Project Management process

The basic process of project management

Five basic management processes that serve to organize the activities are:

Planning- devising a workable scheme to accomplish an objective

Executing- carrying out the plan

Controlling— measuring progress and taking corrective action when necessary

Initiating— setting overall project direction and defining project objectives

Closing— formalizing acceptance of the product of the project and bringing the project itself to an end

Step-by-Step Project Management Process:

6 Steps Approach to Effective Project Management

  1. Clarity of project requirements, project scope

    2. It is crucial for effective project management to be able to have complete and clear project requirement, right at the beginning and avoid project scope creep during project execution.

  2. Planning schedule, risks, resources
  3. Communication plan - clarity & frequency

    4. Project communication is required for:

    • Informing stakeholder about project plan, scope; review and approvals thereof
    • Keeping team members informed about change in project schedule
    • Being able to highlight issues and risks in project
  4. Kick-off project

    5. Now almost everything looks ready on a paper, it is a time for a project team to jump-in and start working on minute level tasks. Every team member, vendor, customer should be informed and made aware of clear expectations from them:

    • When a specific task is supposed to start
    • Who will do it
    • When it should be completed
    • What information should be updated
  5. Monitor & Control

    6. In order to monitor and control project:

    • You will have to collect progress updates
    • Adhere to the project schedule, cost and keep watching project baseline
    • Follow the mantra Get Things done
    • Check project deliverable for quality
    • Take a regular feedback from team, customers and end users
    • Manage change requests, issues and risks judiciously
  6. Deliver, Close and Review

    7. To get customer confirm successful project deliverable, you can schedule a pre-planned review meeting to get a formal agreement, seek feedback, identify positives and negatives of the whole exercise of delivering projects - project review.

Roles in Project Management

Five Critical Roles in Project Management:

  1. Sponsor
    2.

    Sponsor is responsible for the delivery of planned benefits associated with the project, ensures availability of essential project resources, approves the budget and ensure resolution of issues escalated by the Project Manager.

  2. Project Manager
    3.

    Responsible for developing, in conjunction with the Project Sponsor.

    Project manager ensures that the project is delivered on time, to budget and to the required quality standard and responsible for managing the work of consultants, allocating and utilising resources in an efficient manner and maintaining a co-operative, motivated and successful team.

  3. Team Leader
    4.

    Team Leader develops a strategy the team will use to reach its goal, communicates clear instructions to team members and listens their's feedback; creates reports to update the company on the team's progress and distribute to the appropriate personnel.

  4. Team Members
    5.

    The staff who actively work on the project, at some stage, during the lifetime of the project. They provide functional expertise in an administrative process, analyse current and future processes/systems and defining requirements for reporting and interfacing.

  5. The Project Steering Committee
    6.

    The Steering Committee’s role is to provide advice on the budget, defining andrealising outcomes, monitoring risks, quality and timelines, making policy andresourcing decisions, and assessing requests for changes to the scope of the project.

What is IT Project Management

IT project management involves a structured approach to planning, organizing, leading, and controlling information technology projects. It includes overseeing projects for software development, hardware installations, network upgrades, cloud computing and virtualization rollouts, business analytics and data management projects and implementing IT services.

IT project manager is a professional charged with overseeing the process of planning, executing and delegating responsibilities around an organization's information technology (IT) pursuits and goals.

Agile Project Management

Agile is a project management methodology that uses short development cycles called sprints to focus on continuous improvement in the development of a product or service.

Agile Project Management reduces complexity by breaking down the many-months-long cycle of building requirements for the whole project, building the entire product and then testing to find product flaws. Instead small, usable segments of the software product owner are specified, developed and tested in manageable, two to four week cycles.

Agile software development refers to a group of software development methodologies based on iterative development, where requirements and solutions evolve through collaboration between self-organizing cross-functional teams.

Beginner's Guide to Agile with Scrum

Scrum is an iterative method that belongs in the agile camp of how to manage and run projects. It is a framework enables self-organizing groups by promoting strong communication between the team members along with a few disciplines inside the project.

Scrum starts with a Product Owner.

Product owner is typically a project's key stakeholder. Part of the product owner responsibilities is to have a vision of what he or she wishes to build, and convey that vision to the scrum team.

That Product Owner is in charge of making the Product Backlog, a wish list of all of the user requirements that is expected to be completed.

Next up is the Sprint.

The Scrum model suggests that each and every sprint begins with a short planning meeting and ends with a review and retrospective.

During sprint planning, the team pulls a small chunk from the top of that wish list- Sprint Backlog and decides how to implement those pieces.

Teams meet every day to give progress updates in the Daily Scrum.

At the end of the sprint, the work should be Potentially Shippable: ready to hand to a customer, put on a store shelf, or show to a stakeholder.

Scrum Master keeps the team focused on its goal and also ensures the procedure is followed, eliminates impediments, and safeguards the team from disturbances.

Scrum Team is a self-organizing and a cross-functional team, who do the analysis, implementation, design, testing, etc.

As the next sprint begins, the team chooses another chunk of the product backlog and begins working again, until the project is deemed complete.

Implementation of Scrum in 7 Easy Steps

  1. Define your first Scrum Team
    2.

    The team is comprised of 5-9 members.

  2. Define your Sprint length
    3.

    A sprint is a time-box that lasts between 7 and 30 days, and typically it remains the same length for the duration of a project.

  3. Appoint a Scrum Master

  4. Appoint the Product Owner

  5. Create the Initial Product Backlog
    6.

    The Product backlog MUST be prioritized. The most important story should be in the top of the list, so the entire backlog is ranked in order based on story importance.

  6. Plan and Start your First Sprint
    7.

    The team now picks items from the list, brainstorms and decides on what and how much they can complete in the upcoming sprint. This is called the sprint planning meeting.

  7. Close the Current and Start the Next Sprint
    8.

    When the end of the time-box is reached all planned work should hopefully be done. If this is not the case it's up to the team to decide if the remaining work should transfer to the next sprint or be put back into the backlog.
